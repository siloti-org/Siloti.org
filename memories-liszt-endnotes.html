Endnotes

with links to recordings & resources

[1] Moscow Conservatory, founded 1866 by Nikolai Rubinstein. His brother, Anton Rubinstein, had founded the contrasting St. Petersburg Conservatory in 1862. The Moscow tradition (Tchaikovsky, Arensky, Taneyev) was seen as more traditional and cosmopolitan, while St. Petersburg (Balakirev, Rimsky-Korsakov, Borodin, Mussorgsky) became more daringly idiosyncratic and with a focused nationalist slant (Rachmaninoff & Riesemann 1934).

[2] Nikolai Rubinstein (1835-1881). Pianist, conductor and pedagogue. Founded Moscow Conservatory 1866. Advocate of the young Tchaikovsky. Premiered Balakirev's Islamey. Teacher of Sergei Taneyev, Alexander Siloti, and Emil von Sauer. (Garden 2001).

[3] Franz Liszt (1811-1886). Pianist, composer, teacher, conductor. His myriad works range from rapid flare etudes to poetic solos and symphonic poems. He advanced possibilities for the piano and symphonic forms in technical possibilities, harmonic color, and expression of poetic essence, and developed the technique of thematic transformation.

[4] Nikolai Zverev (1833-1893). Foundations teacher of Siloti, Rachmaninoff, and Scriabin. Trusted by the advanced piano faculty at Moscow Conservatory to prepare younger pupils with a strong foundation before handing them off to the advanced teachers. (Rachmaninoff & Riesemann 1934).

[5] Liszt's Reminiscences de Norma de Bellini — a solo piano concert paraphrase on themes from Bellini's opera. Recording by Furruccio Busoni.

[6] Liszt's Totentanz: Paraphrase on Dies irae — a concerto-like symphonic poem for piano and orchestra. Highly complex and demanding emotionally and pianistically. A letter by Liszt (5 Dec 1883; Williams 1998) suggests few pianists were able to achieve a compelling performance in these times -- but Siloti did. This work resurfaced in significant moments for Siloti (it was the final work assigned to him by N. Rubinstein, his Moscow Conservatory graduation piece, what he performed in the first Liszt Concert at Leipzig 1883, and final work performed in Carnegie Hall, NY 1929; Downes 1929). N. Rubinstein likely knew that Liszt favored this work and assigned it to Siloti expecting it would aid Siloti's introduction. Recording by Liszt student Jose Vianna da Motta.

[7] Sergei Taneyev (1856-1915). Composer, theorist, extensive investigator of harmony and counterpoint. Professor of composition at Moscow Conservatory (pupils included Rachmaninoff and Scriabin). Deeply respected by Rachmaninoff (Rachmaninoff & Riesemann 1934).

[8] Father Dimitri Razumovsky (1818-1889). Priest & professor of Church music at Moscow Conservatory. Advised Tchaikovsky on traditional choral & liturgical composition (Tchaikovsky Research 2023) and helped pioneer the scholarly research of Russian chant (Duckles et al. 2001).

[9] Ivan Turgenev (1818-1883). Renowned Russian Novelist. Captured enduring portraits of Russian peasantry and intelligentsia intent on their search for ways to better society. Turgenev visited N. Rubinstein in Paris, cared for him like"a close relative," and helped transport his coffin back to Moscow (N. Rubinstein in Tchaikovsky 1881).

[10] Imperial Russian Musical Society. Established by A. Rubinstein and Count Matv. Y. Vielgorsky in 1859. Mission to deepen musical appreciation and education for the people and help enable local composers and performers through concerts, competitions, grants, and opportunity for publication and performance of emerging works (Porfiryeva n.d.).

[11] Anton Rubinstein (1829-1894). Renowned as a legendary pianist throughout Europe. Performing style noted for more spontaneity compared to his elder brother Nikolai. Rachmaninoff highly esteemed Rubinstein's piano tone (Rachmaninoff 1913). Prolific composer. Pivotal in laying foundations for Russian musical education. Founded Moscow Musical Society in 1859 and St. Petersburg Conservatory 1862.

[12] Schumann's Kreisleriana (1838-9). Recording by Grigory Sokolov.

[13] Beethoven Piano Concerto No. 5 in E flat, "Emperor." One of the final three piano and orchestral works Siloti would perform in his 1929 New York recital (Downes 1929).

[14] Beethoven Sonata op. 101 in A (1816). Beethoven's 28th piano sonata, demonstrates his late period trends towards lyricism and inward, delicate passages (Kerman et al. 2001). Recording by Emil Gilels.

[15] Johannes Kreisler. A fictional character created by E.T.A. Hoffmann who distilled and impersonated musical aesthetics of the Romantic Era–including the heightened stature of music as a metaphysical art beyond words, and the importance of the artist-composer (Moraal 1994).

[16] Max Erdmannsdorfer (1848-1905). A German conductor, pianist, and composer, who in 1882 became principal conductor of the Moscow Russian Musical Society Concerts and professor at Moscow Conservatory. Tchaikovsky entrusted him with the premiere of certain of his works, despite his concerns for Erdmannsdorfer's tendency to "to indulge the public's taste of exaggerated nuances" and his "offhanded in his attitude to Russian music (except my own)" (Tchaikovsky 1882).

[17] Allgemeine Deutsche Musikverein. Musical society representing the New German School. Founded by Liszt and Franz Brendel in 1861 to support the performance of new, important, and rarely heard works. Liszt suggested much innovative repertoire, including many works by a range of Russian composers (Kaminiarz 1995).

[18] Fraulein Marie Lipsius (1837-1927). Writer and music historian with the pseudonym "La Mara". Close friend of Liszt and princess Carolyne zu Sayn-Wittgenstein. Wrote a series of artful biographical sketches of composers and compiled two volumes of Liszt's letters (see these links for English translations: Vol.1 and Vol. 2).

[19] Hans von Bulow (1830-1894). Virtuoso pianist, early student of Liszt, conductor and composer. Later became Liszt's son-in-law after marrying his daughter Cosima. Premiered Liszt's Sonata in B minor and Totentanz. Promoted Tchaikovsky Concerto No. 1 after Nikolai Rubinstein's criticism and before Siloti's edits.

[20] Chopin's Ballade No. 3 in A flat Major (1841). Recording by Alfred Corot.

[21] Paris Conservatoire. Founded by the French state in 1795. Established a beneficial free music library that was curated by Hector Berlioz from the 1830s through 1869 (Tayeb & Austin n.d.). Cherubini became director in 1822. He increased the rigor of training towards composition, requiring counterpoint, harmony, and fugue in addition to foundational piano skills. (Cook et al. 2001).

[22] Liszt Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2 in C-sharp Minor (pub. 1851). Recording by Marc-Andre Hamelin. Liszt collaborated with his pupil Franz Doppler to orchestrate a series of Rhapsodies. Recording of the orchestral version by the Berliner Philharmoniker and Herbert von Karajan.

[23] Beethoven's Piano Sonata No. 14 in C-sharp Minor, Quasi una fantasia, (1802). Widely known by it's nickname, the "moonlight" sonata. Recording by Siloti student Alexander Goldenweiser.

[24] Chopin Scherzo in No. 2 in B-flat Minor (1835-7). Recording by Mikhail Pletnev.

[25] Chopin's Preludes. Chopin composed a cycle of preludes 24 in all keys and three standalone publications. Recording of the 24 preludes by Vladimir Ashkenazy.

[26] "quand meme" — a common French expression with subtle range of meanings. In this context, an English equivalent may be "nevertheless" with a nuance of slight exasperation.

[27] Georges Sand was the pen name of French novelist Amantine Lucile Aurore Dupin de Francueil (1804-1876). She was prolific and exceedingly popular during her time, yet was known to behave in socially rebellious ways (such as smoking or wearing men's clothing in public). She had a number of problematic romantic affairs with a range of artists, including Chopin and the dramatist Musset.

[28] Liszt's Faust Symphony (1853). Recording by Leonard Bernstein.

[29] Arthur Friedheim (1859-1932). German pianist, conductor and composer born in St. Petersburg. Studied with Anton Rubinstein for one year, but like Siloti, went instead to Liszt. Liszt's pupil and secretary for the final eight years. A sensitive pianist with exquisite technique, Friedheim also authors many written reflections in Liszt, compiled by his student into the book Life and Liszt(New York, 1961). Unfortunately, few of his musical compositions were published and scarce manuscripts survive.

[30] Alfred Reisenauer (1863-1907). His parents presented him to Liszt at twelve years old, and he soon became one of Liszt's favorite pupils. Went on to become professor then director at Leipzig Conservatory.

[31] "vis a vis" — French term meaning "opposite" or "facing each other"

[32] Rubinstein Sonata for Piano Four Hands in D Major, Op. 89. Recording by Bakhchirev and Sorokina (Mov. 1, Mov. 2, Mov. 3)

[33] Schubert-Liszt Wander Fantasy. Liszt transformed Schubert's solo piano work into an epic work for piano and orchestra. Recording by Jorge Bolet and the London Symphony Orchestra.

[34] Liszt's Consolations, six poetic works for solo piano. Recording by Lilya Zilberstein.

[35] Liszt's Ballade von Burger, Melodrama Lenore (1857-1858) for piano and narrator. Inspired by Gottfried August Burger's 1773 poem. Recording by Martha Argerich and Annie Dutoit.

[36] In 1885-86, Anton Rubinstein performed an epic series of "Historical Concerts" around Russia and Europe. These seven recitals spanned an impressive breadth of time and style. This endeavor aligned with Rubinstein's broader objective to help people appreciate musical masters and their works. Rachmaninoff comments on the marvel of these concerts and how they helped the audience connect with composers like Bach at a new depth and sincerity (Rachmaninoff 1913).

[37] Gewandhaus. Concert hall in Leipzig. Home of the Gewandhaus orchestra, directed by Felix Mendelssohn 1835-47.

[38] Daniel Liszt (1839-1859). One of three children (Cosima, Blandine, and Daniel) between Liszt and the author Marie d'Agoult, whose pen name was Daniel Stern. Their only son Daniel died of tuberculosis at age 20, leaving a deep impact on Liszt and his music. Siloti also would suffer the death of a son, losing his boy Ivan at only age seven (Barber 2002).

[39] Liszt Hungarian Rhapsody No. 14 in F Minor (1852). Recording by Ignaz Friedman.

[40] Liszt Piano Concerto No. 2 in A Major (1839-1840). Recording by Sviatoslav Richter and the London Symphony Orchestra under Kirill Kondrashin.

[41] Liszt's Apres une lecture du Dante: Fantasia quasi Sonata for solo piano (1849) after the poem by Victor Hugo. Recording by Mikhail Pletnev.

[42] "parterre" — German for "ground floor"

[43] William Humphries Dayas (1864-1903). "A fellow-pupil of Liszt's, Dayas was also one of Siloti's greatest friends." (Oxana Siloti 1979)

[44] Arthur Nikisch (1855-1922). Conductor. A friend and collaborator of Siloti in many St. Petersburg Siloti Concerts (Oxana Siloti 1979).

[45] Richard Wagner (1813-1883). Along with Liszt, represented the New German School in the Romantic Era. Envisioned a "total artwork" of music, words, theatre, light, artwork which he sought to realize in his operas, for which he was librettist and composer. His compositions showcase daring harmonic explorations and strategic use leitmotifs to link music with thematic or characteristic elements onstage.

[46] Julius Bluthner (1824-1910). Siloti's chosen piano manufacturer. Bluthner patented a repetition action and developed pianos prized for their "round, slightly romantic tone, with a particularly full treble."(Cranmer 2001). Bluthner tested every piano by hand until he passed this duty onto his sons. Provided much financial support to fund Siloti's endeavors. Oxana remembers, "Bluthner supported my father generously at the beginning of his career. Julius Bluthner was the founder and head of the firm that manufactured the pianos Siloti played on." (Oxana Siloti 1979)

[47] Martin Krause (1835-1918) Pianist, teacher, writer and music critic. Pupil of Liszt who later systematically studied Liszt's method and teaching (Methuen-Campbell 2001).

[48] Wagner's final opera Parsifal (1882). Recording by the Bayreuth Festival Chorus and Orchestra.

[49] Joseph Joachim (1831-1907). Virtuoso violinist and composer. Admired Liszt's music greatly. Liszt admired Joachim's musicality, writing "You do not number among those artist that one can replace–and no one appreciates more than I all the strength of your talent, your character and individuality" (Liszt to Joachim in 1853 as translated in Uhde 2018).

[50] "Stimmung" — German for mood, sentiment, or atmosphere

[51] Cosima Wagner (1837-1930). Daughter of Liszt and Marie d'Agoult. Married the pianist Hans con Bulow in 1857 but divorced him for Wagner in 1870. She founded Bayreuth festival with Wagner and became the core artistic director after his death in 1883.

[52] Alexander Glazunov (1865-1936). Oxana Siloti remembers, "Glazunov was a great personal friend of both my father and my mother. He dedicated his suite 'From the Middle Ages' to my mother, and in it there are musical portraits of my parents, as the lovers in the castle, and of himself, as the troubadour at the gates. Glazunov spoke so softly that it was often hard to hear him. A favorite story is about his saying to the musicians of the orchestra at a rehearsal, barely audibly: 'Please, gentlemen, fortissimo!'" (Oxana Siloti 1979).

[53] Alexander Borodin (1833-1887). Distinguished doctor, passionate chemist and imaginative Russian composer. One of the Russian Five circle of early nationalist composers. Encouraged by Balakirev and Liszt, who during a visit "insisted on playing through both his symphonies with him in four-hand piano arrangements. Liszt's verdict and his advice to Borodin was, 'You are always lucid, intelligent and perfectly original & Work in your own way and pay no attention to anyone'" (Liszt in Oldani 2001).

[54] Carl Tausig (1841-1871). Virtuoso pianist, arranger. Pupil and friend of Liszt. Arranged Liszt's Faust Symphony for piano solo (see recording by Istvan Lajko).

[55] Madame Sophie Menter (1846-1918). Pupil of Liszt before Siloti. Taught at St. Petersburg Conservatory. After Liszt's death, toured Russia in concert with Siloti. (Barber 2002).

[56] St. Petersburg Conservatory. Founded by Anton Rubinstein, who taught there alongside composer Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov. Seen as more revolutionary and nationalistic in contrast to the more cosmopolitan Moscow conservatory (Rachmaninoff & Riesemann 1934).

[57] Felix Mottl (1856-1911). Fellow pupil of Liszt. Austrian conductor and composer and orchestrator. Regarded as a brilliant conductor and entrusted with many of Wagner's daring Bayreuth premieres. Worked with Arthur Nikisch at Leipzig Opera. Liszt supported the production of Mottl's works (Miller 2001), and Mottl would go on to support other emerging performances (New York Times, 1903). Mottl orchestrated, edited, and conducted for Siloti's St. Petersburg Concerts.
